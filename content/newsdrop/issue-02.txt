📜 Something Old
For two decades, marketing effectiveness had one gospel: The attention economy. Mental availability. Be easy to recall at the moment of choice. Byron Sharp said it. The industry built on it. It worked. Ben Essen, global CSO at Iris, argues the foundations are cracking. AI systems now curate the shortlist before a human even engages. Search gives answers instead of options. “Advantage shifts from recall to reputation,” Essen suggests. Google itself is hedging against its own ad model: AI Overviews correlate with a 58% drop in click-through rates for top-ranking pages. His closing is profound: "The future of brand isn't being remembered. It's being recommended." → LINK
🆕 Something New
Anthropic is on a roll! And the pattern is clear: every new feature pushes Claude further into autonomous, agentic territory. The latest update introduces computer use for all Pro and Max subscribers as a research preview - although currently still macOS only. 🤝 Something Borrowed
Merriam-Webster named "slop" its 2025 Word of the Year, and AI Fruit Love Island is the poster child: objectively low-quality content - choppy edits, mismatched lip-sync, nonsensical plotlines. And yet: nearly 300 million views, 23 million likes, and individual episodes clearing 10 million in under two weeks. One TikToker put it plainly: "All it has taken for people to switch their opinion on AI is seeing an AI strawberry and an AI orange having relationship drama." So is this enslopification? Or is simply, as Forbes puts it, driving “massive engagement”? For marketers, the uncomfortable answer is: probably both. The whole affair raises uncomfortable questions about content, quality, and engagement. → LINK

💙 Something Blue (no villain, just a feeling)
Sora is no more... Six months after launching a standalone app that topped the App Store, OpenAI announced it's shutting the whole thing down. App, API, website. Gone. The Disney deal is off too. The $1 billion investment and 200-character licensing agreement from December? No money ever changed hands. Disney said it "respects OpenAI's decision to exit the video generation business." Why now? Compute costs. OpenAI said it needed to make trade-offs on products with high resource demands as it pivots toward enterprise, coding and a planned IPO. Downloads had already dropped sharply from 3.3 million in November to 1.1 million by February. The platform dependency risk is well-documented in the AI space. Few expected to see it materialise quite this abruptly. Runway, Kling, Pika and LTX remain. The tools aren't going away. This one just did. → LINK
